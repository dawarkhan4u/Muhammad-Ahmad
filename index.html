BEFORE: Massive fanbase, email not keeping up. Campaigns sent to the full list with no strategy. Low open rates, low conversion. AFTER: $385K attributed to email. 53% of total store revenue. 257% growth.

THE SITUATION

BioWare Gear had something most brands only dream about — a massive, deeply passionate fanbase built on two decades of beloved game franchises. Dragon Age. Mass Effect. These weren't casual buyers; they were collectors, loyalists, people who wanted to own a piece of the universes they loved. The demand was already there. The email list was sizeable. But the email strategy wasn't remotely close to what the audience deserved or what the business needed.

Campaigns went to the full list with no segmentation — Mass Effect fans getting Dragon Age promotions, lapsed buyers getting the same messages as new subscribers. Open rates were average. Conversion was low. The automated flows were either missing entirely or too generic to resonate with a fandom audience. Post-purchase experiences didn't reinforce community or drive second purchases. There was a significant gap between how much this audience wanted to engage with the brand and how little the email channel was facilitating that engagement.

WHAT I DID

The first thing I rebuilt was the segmentation architecture. With a fandom brand, generic blasts are almost insulting — the audience self-identifies by the franchise they love, and every send needed to respect that. I built franchise-based segments, purchase-history segments, and engagement tiers so every campaign went to exactly the right people with content they'd actually care about.

Then I built the flow ecosystem from the ground up. New product launch sequences timed around game release cycles and merchandise drops, with copy written to speak directly to the fan identity — not just "buy this item" but "this is for you, the person who replayed Dragon Age three times." Post-purchase flows that leaned into community and lore, driving repeat purchases by deepening the relationship between buyer and brand. Win-back campaigns that referenced specific purchase history to re-engage dormant fans. Browse abandonment flows with copy that acknowledged what they were looking at and why it mattered to someone in their specific fandom.

The result was an email channel that finally matched the energy of the audience. Every send had a reason to exist — a specific segment, a specific message, a specific moment in the customer journey.

THE OUTCOME

$385,000 in email-attributed revenue — 53% of the store's total revenue generated through the email channel alone. 257% growth. The store did $726K total; email accounted for over half of it. That's what happens when you match the strategy to the audience.

THE SITUATION

An eCommerce brand with a solid product, a healthy list, and results that were average at best. Open rates sat in the mid-twenties — technically not bad, but not indicative of an engaged audience. Click rates were low. Revenue per recipient was negligible. Klaviyo's health ratings were middling across all four metrics. The problem wasn't the product, and it wasn't the list — it was the strategy. Every campaign went to everyone, regardless of engagement level, purchase history, or where they were in the customer journey. The list was slowly burning out.

WHAT I DID

I started with a full list audit before sending a single campaign. Cleaned the list first — removed unengageable contacts, sunset inactive subscribers, and rebuilt the segments from the ground up based on engagement tier, purchase frequency, and category interest. Only after the list was clean did I start sending — and when I did, every campaign went to a defined segment, not the full list.

The content strategy shifted to match. Each send had a specific audience and a specific reason to land in their inbox that day. Subject lines were written for the segment, not a generic catch-all. I ran 7 campaigns in 30 days — not carpet-bombing the full list, but precision sends that built momentum. Open rates went up with each send, not down. That's the tell-tale sign of a list that's being treated right.

THE OUTCOME

60.4% open rate — nearly double the industry average of 25–35%. 4 out of 4 Excellent ratings on Klaviyo's health dashboard, across deliverability, engagement, growth, and compliance. Revenue per recipient hit $0.50 — small number, but across a healthy list it compounds fast. The list didn't just perform better; it became an asset again.
